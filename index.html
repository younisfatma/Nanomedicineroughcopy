Nanomedicine

Nanomedicine has opened up entirely new avenues for treatments. This branch of medicine involves the use of nanoscale materials, such as biocompatible nanoparticles and nanobots, for diagnosis, delivery, sensing or actuation purposes in a living organism. Nanobots, a molecule-sized nanoparticle, can be used to treat many new diseases and improve the human biological system. Due to their precision, minimum side effects and quicker response nanobots have been found more innovative and supportive of the treatment and diagnosis of cancer, heart attack, diabetes, atherosclerosis, and kidney stones. Through regular monitoring of the body’s condition, nanobots allow a personalized treatment and thus achieve high efficiency against many diseases.

What are Nanobots?

Nanobots are tiny molecule sized particles. They are programed to complete very specific tasks and are designed from the biological model of bacteria. Carbon is the principal element used in constructing most anobots. To construct the outershell of the nanobot diamondoid material is used, due to its inert properties, high thermal conductivity and strength. This super smooth surface helps reduce the chance of triggering the body’s immune system. Bearing, gears, and motors make up the mechanical part of a nanobot. These parts are constructed using elements like hydrogen, sulphur, oxygen, nitrogen or silicon. Furthermore, the substructures of nanobots are built with onboard power supplies, sensors, nanocomputers, pumps, manipulators,and pressure tanks. In recent years scientists have found ways to apply these machines into medicine for treatment of diseases.

History of Nanotechnology

Nanotechnology has quickly advanced in the past decade, and scientists believe it to be the future of medicine. Physicist Richard Feynman was the first to speak about the ideas and concepts behind nanoscience and nanotechnology. His talk entitled “There’s Plenty of Room at the Bottom” at an American Physical Society meeting in the California Institute of Technology on December 29, 1959, is considered to be the first lecture on technology and engineering at the atomic scale. Long before the term Nanotechnology was coined, he described a process in which scientists could manipulate and control individual atoms and molecules. The term Nanotechnology originated a decade later during Professor Norio Taniguchi’s exploration of ultra-precision machining at Tokyo Science University. Professor Taniguchi used the term nanotechnology to describe the precision machining of materials within atomic-scale dimensional tolerances. The advancement of modern nanotechnology only began after The Scanning Tunneling microscope that could “see” individual atoms was developed in 1981 by Gerd Binning and Heinrich Rohrer. Since then research in Nanotechnology has boomed. Nanotechnology is being used in many everyday items, such as, lightweight nanotechnology-enabled automobile bumpers that resist denting and scratching, golf balls that fly straighter, stiffer and stronger tennis rackets, nano-silver antibacterials socks, clear sunscreens and improved television, cell phones, and digital camera displays. Research and advancements in Nanotechnology are predicted to grow in all areas from your cell phone to nanobots used for surgical purposes.
